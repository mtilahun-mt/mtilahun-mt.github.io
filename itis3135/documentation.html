Introduction

Welcome, young brewer. The Miraculous Turtle has wandered this earth for over 500 years, and in that time has distilled the art of tea into five core principles. This manual is the result of that long and patient study.

Tea is not merely a beverage — it is a meditation. The act of brewing teaches patience, precision, and presence. A great cup of tea cannot be rushed.

This documentation covers everything you need to brew like an ancient turtle: equipment selection, water quality, temperature, steeping times, and the deeper techniques that separate a good cup from a legendary one.

/* The First Law of Tea */ Brew slowly. Taste thoughtfully. Repeat for 500 years.
  • Each tea type has its own ideal temperature range.
  • Water quality is just as important as leaf quality.
  • The correct steep time is the difference between balance and bitterness.
Equipment

The right tools are the foundation of great tea. You do not need expensive equipment to brew well — but you do need the right equipment for the job.

The Miraculous Turtle's recommended toolkit has three tiers: the minimum you need to start, the standard home setup, and the advanced configuration for serious practitioners.

/* Minimum Required Equipment */ 1 × Kettle with temperature control 1 × Teapot or gaiwan (100–150 ml ideal) 1 × Fine-mesh strainer 1 × Timer
  • Kettle: Variable temperature is essential. Never guess at heat.
  • Vessel: Unglazed Yixing clay for oolong and pu-erh; glass or porcelain for everything else.
  • Strainer: Mesh fine enough to catch broken leaves and dust.
  • Timer: A phone timer works. Precision matters more than elegance.
  • Scale: Weigh your leaves. Volume measurements are unreliable.
Water and Temperature

Water is 99% of your cup. The Miraculous Turtle once ruined a 400-year-old Pu-erh cake by brewing it with tap water. Do not make the same mistake.

Use filtered water with moderate mineral content (80–150 ppm TDS). Distilled water is too flat; hard tap water introduces off-flavours and scale.

/* Temperature Reference (Celsius) */ White Tea: 68–72 °C Green Tea: 72–80 °C Oolong: 85–95 °C (depends on oxidation level) Black Tea: 95–100 °C Pu-erh: 95–100 °C Herbal: 95–100 °C
  • Never boil water more than once — reoxygenate by pouring between vessels.
  • Warm your teapot before brewing by rinsing with hot water.
  • If your kettle lacks temperature control, let boiling water rest 2 min for ~85 °C, 5 min for ~75 °C.
Steeping Guide

Steep time is the variable most beginners get wrong. Too short and your tea is thin and empty. Too long and it becomes bitter and astringent. Both are tragedies the Turtle has witnessed too many times.

The ratios below assume 3 g of dry leaf per 150 ml of water. Adjust leaf quantity, not steep time, when scaling volume.

/* Steeping Reference — Western Style */ White Tea: 2–4 min Green Tea: 1–2 min (re-steep 2–3 times) Oolong: 2–5 min (re-steep 4–6 times) Black Tea: 3–5 min Pu-erh: 30 sec–3 min per infusion Herbal: 5–7 min (no re-steeping)
  • Remove leaves promptly when time is up. Never leave them sitting in water.
  • Green and oolong teas reward multiple short infusions over a single long one.
  • For Gongfu-style: reduce steep time by 60–70% and increase leaf quantity by 50%.
Advanced Techniques

The following techniques require patience and repetition to master. The Turtle spent 40 years perfecting his Gongfu pour alone. Do not be discouraged by slow progress — in tea as in life, the journey is the point.

These practices will improve the quality of any tea, but they will make the most difference with high-quality loose-leaf materials.

/* Gongfu Cha — Short-Infusion Technique */ Leaf-to-water: 6–8 g per 100 ml Infusion 1: Rinse (5 sec — discard) Infusion 2: 20–30 sec Infusion 3: 25–35 sec Infusion 4+: Add 5–10 sec per round Peak infusion: Usually 4th–7th /* Cold Brew (Green or White) */ Leaves: 3 g per 500 ml cold filtered water Time: 8–12 hours in refrigerator Strain: Fine mesh before serving Result: Naturally sweet, low bitterness
  • Rinsing: A quick first rinse awakens the leaf and removes dust. Always discard the rinse water.
  • Decanting: Pour all liquid out of the vessel at the end of each infusion — never leave tea sitting.
  • Warming: Pre-warm cups with hot water for even temperature throughout the session.
  • Observation: Watch the colour of the liquor change across infusions. It tells you when the leaf is exhausted.
  • Notes: Keep a brewing journal. Track leaf, water, temperature, time, and tasting notes. Review it after 500 years.
