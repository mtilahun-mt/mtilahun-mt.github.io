Major Ethiopian Orthodox Holidays

Click a holiday name to expand its description.

Amharic: ገና Date: January 7

Gena celebrates the Nativity of Jesus Christ. Unlike Western traditions, Gena is observed with solemnity rather than gift-giving. The celebration begins with an all-night vigil on January 6, with the main liturgy on January 7. Men and boys traditionally play a stick game also called gena, believed to have been played by shepherds on the very night of the Nativity.

Amharic: ጥምቀት Date: January 19

Timkat celebrates the Epiphany — the baptism of Jesus Christ in the Jordan River. It is the most colorful and joyful holiday in the Ethiopian Orthodox calendar. The Tabot (replica of the Ark of the Covenant) is carried in solemn procession to a body of water, where the faithful renew their baptismal vows. Priests wear elaborate ceremonial robes and the celebration continues for three days.

Amharic: ፋሲካ Date: Varies (spring)

Fasika is the celebration of the Resurrection of Jesus Christ and is the holiest day in the Ethiopian Orthodox calendar. It is preceded by the 55-day Lenten fast (Abiy Tsom) during which no animal products are consumed. The celebration begins after midnight with the breaking of the fast, joyful hymns, and a great feast. Traditional dishes include doro wat (spiced chicken stew) and freshly baked injera bread.

Amharic: እንቁጣጣሽ Date: September 11

Enkutatash marks the Ethiopian New Year, which falls at the end of the rainy season when the highlands bloom with yellow Adey Abeba flowers. Children carry bouquets of these flowers and sing traditional songs from house to house. The name means “gift of jewels,” referring to the legend that the Queen of Sheba received jewels upon her return from Jerusalem. A special liturgy is held and families gather for a traditional feast.

Amharic: መስቀል Date: September 27

Meskel commemorates the discovery of the True Cross by Empress Helena in the 4th century. Ethiopian tradition holds that a large portion of the True Cross was brought to Ethiopia and is preserved at Gishen Mariam monastery. The celebration is marked by the lighting of a great bonfire called Damera, around which priests and the faithful dance. It is one of the most visually spectacular celebrations in the Ethiopian calendar.

Amharic: ልደታ Date: August 30 (Nehase 19)

Lideta celebrates the birth of the Virgin Mary, who holds a uniquely exalted position in Ethiopian Orthodox theology. Our own congregation — Bata Lemariam (House of Mary) — is dedicated to her. The day is observed with special liturgy, fasting, and prayers in her honor. The 21st of every Ethiopian month is also dedicated to the Virgin Mary.

Amharic: ኪዳነ ምሕረት Date: February 16 (Yekatit 9)

Kidane Mehret commemorates the covenant (Kidane) made between the Virgin Mary and Christ before His Ascension, in which He promised mercy to all who honor her. This feast is especially significant in our tradition and is one of the major feasts of the Virgin Mary. Churches dedicated in her name celebrate it with particular solemnity.

Amharic: ቡሄ Date: August 19 (Nehase 13)

Buhe celebrates the Transfiguration of Jesus Christ on Mount Tabor. The night before Buhe, young boys light torches, sing traditional songs (Hoya Hoye), and go from house to house collecting bread. Large bonfires are lit to symbolize the divine light that shone at the Transfiguration. A special liturgy is held and the faithful break a fasting period with the traditional Dabo (ceremonial bread).
